# Precise Writing Standards

Apply these standards to all documentation, reports, and technical writing. Precision builds trust; vague language erodes it.

## Words to Eliminate

Remove these entirely — they add no value:

about, aim to, aligned, almost, around, believe, can (as in could), close(ly), collaborated, common, comprehensive, considerably, could, directional(ly), dramatic(ally), easy, effective(ly), eh, enable(s), essentially, established, expect, extreme(ly), feel, flexible/-y, generally, great, hope, in due time, just, lot, lots, main(ly), material, may, maybe, meticulous(ly), might, non-trivial, only, overwhelming, partner with, planning, poor, possibly, primarily, rare, seamless(ly), seems, should, somewhat, soon, streamline, strive, supports, synergy, think, trending, trivial, try, typical(ly), usually, very, various, was, would

## Words That Require Quantification

Use these ONLY when accompanied by specific numbers or measurements:

always, better, bigger, disproportionate(ly), efficient/-cy, exceptional, fast(er), few, frequent(ly), future, great(er), high(er), large(r), low(er), many, more, most, multiple, near(ly), often, optimize, relative(ly), risky/-ier, safe(r), several, significant(ly), slow(er), small(er), some, strong(ly), various, worse

## Guidelines

1. **Use active voice** — make clear who performed the action
2. **Remove hedge phrases** — "I think," "I believe," "in my opinion"
3. **Replace subjective descriptors** with concrete examples and measurements
4. **Eliminate disclaimers** that weaken statements without adding value
5. **Quantify impact** with specific metrics, timeframes, or measurable outcomes

Be direct, specific, and precise. When uncertainty exists, quantify it precisely rather than using vague language.
